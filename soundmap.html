Overview

Soundmap is a map-based music experience that treats place as the primary interface for discovery. Instead of browsing playlists, you explore neighborhoods.

Most music products optimize for personalization. Soundmap prioritizes context instead. The premise: every neighborhood has a distinct sound identity shaped by local scenes, venues, time of day, and the people moving through it. Those identities should be explorable — and worth going to.

The Idea

Most music apps answer one question. Soundmap asks a different one.

Most apps

"What should I listen to?"

Soundmap

"What does this place sound like?"

The shift is from feed-based discovery — algorithmic, passive, personalized — to spatial discovery: contextual, active, place-anchored.

The Interaction

Every place has two states.

Preview

Accessible from anywhere

Generalized and limited. A taste of the place — enough to understand its character, not enough to replace being there.

Live

Unlocked on arrival

Richer and more specific. Smaller artists, denser activity, higher signal — because you earned it by showing up.

This creates a deliberate tradeoff: accessibility vs depth. Users can explore from anywhere, but the full experience requires presence. Discovery becomes something you move toward.

The System

The same interaction produces different outcomes depending on place. That's the point.

The system adapts to context, not just preference.

Bushwick: dense, chaotic, discovery-heavy. SoHo: minimal, structured, familiar. The UI reflects this — map density, tracklist depth, and content tone all shift with the neighborhood's identity, not with a user's listening history.

Decisions

  • Soft unlock — preview anywhere, unlock on arrival
    Keeps the app useful even without physical presence, while preserving the reward for showing up. Without a preview, the map is a wall. Without an unlock, presence has no payoff.
  • Neighborhood-level identity — not tied to exact GPS
    Geofencing at the block level creates fragmentation and anxiety. Neighborhood-level unlocks feel natural to how people think about place and move through cities.
  • Contextual pins — each track carries meaning
    Pins require a short note to post. Without context, the map becomes noise. A single sentence keeps quality high without adding friction that would kill participation.
  • Hybrid signals — user, curated, and behavioral
    Relying entirely on user pins produces inconsistency — high-traffic places like SoHo flood with pins while quieter neighborhoods go silent. Relying entirely on editorial curation produces rigidity — the map reflects someone's taste, not the place. The hybrid approach (user-generated plus editorially weighted plus behavioral) is how the map stays alive across the full range of neighborhoods. It is the same tension between algorithm and editorial that every discovery product faces.

Scope

Focused on a single behavior: discover music through place.

Included
  • Map exploration
  • Neighborhood previews
  • Arrival unlocks
  • Contextual pins
Excluded
  • Social graph
  • Messaging
  • Events & ticketing
  • Full routes system

Keeping scope tight forced clarity on the core interaction. Social features, events, and routing all exist as natural extensions — but adding them in v1 would have diluted the place-first premise before it had a chance to prove itself.

Reflection

Soundmap explores a different model for music products: spatial instead of feed-based, contextual instead of purely personalized, exploratory instead of optimized.

Every major streaming platform has converged on the same surface — an algorithmic queue that learns your preferences and feeds them back to you. That model is excellent at giving you more of what you already like. It is not designed for the moment you want to find something you don't know yet, tied to a place you're actually in.

The interesting design question isn't whether place-based discovery works — it obviously does, and has for centuries. The question is what it looks like as a product that earns a place in how people actually move through cities and find music.

Music doesn't just live in your library.
It lives in places.
